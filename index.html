Conflict Resolution Workshop for Multicultural Teams

A practical, hands‑on workshop tailored for multicultural teams in Canadian organizations. Focus: clear communication, cultural awareness, and constructive conflict resolution.

  • Identify common conflict sources (bias, language barriers, norms)
  • Practice respectful feedback and repair techniques
  • Role‑play realistic workplace scenarios
Sign-Up

For maximizing workplace cooperation

Tailored for the Canadian work experience, this workshop teaches participants to identify conflicts and resolve them with maximum efficiency. By first teaching participants how to categorize conflict, then secondly guiding them through practice, participants gain both knowledge and experience in this short 90-120 minutes workshop.

Workshop Agenda

An overview of the workshop schedule.

  • Group size: 5+ people
  • Length: 90–120mins
  • Location: Vancouver Community Collage

1. Welcome & Norms
(10 min)

❮
Psychological safety & confidentiality

Creating an environment where everyone feels safe to share without fear of judgment.

Shared expectations

Listen actively, ask questions, and assume positive intent from others.

❯

2. Sources of Conflict
(15 min)

  • Discrimination

    Includes untoward comments based on false biases.

    Sources:

    • Race: Prejudices based on skin color or racial background.
    • Gender: Stereotypes or bias regarding gender roles or identity.
    • Disability: Assumptions about physical or mental capabilities.
    • Physical Appearance: Judgments based on height, weight, or clothing.
    • Language: Barriers due to accents, fluency, or idioms.
    • Ethnicity or Culture: Misunderstandings from differing traditions or values.
    • Nationality or Immigration Status: Bias against foreign-born or non-citizens.

    Groups covered by Employment Equity Act (1995):

    • Women
    • Aboriginal peoples
    • Persons with disabilities
    • Members of visible minorities
  • Language Barrier
    • Limitations of Official Languages Act (1984):
      • Official workplace languages can only be English or French.
      • Enforcement follows provincial law (e.g., BC vs. Quebec).
    • Communication Misunderstandings: Occur when a cohesive language is not present in meetings.
    • Tonal Languages: Can sound aggressive or create misconceptions of anger to non-speakers.

3. Core Skills
(20 min)

  • Nonviolent Communication Practice observing without judgment, sharing feelings, naming needs, and making requests.
  • Curious questions & paraphrasing Ask open-ended questions and repeat back what you heard to ensure understanding.
  • Agree‑on‑next‑steps Conclude discussions with clear, actionable steps for moving forward.

4. Role‑Play Rotations
(30–45 min)

  • Take turns playing 5 prepared characters Engage with different personas to understand diverse perspectives.
  • Rotate roles Switch between being a team leader, developer, and manager to experience different responsibilities.

5 Debrief & Commitments
(15–20 min)

  • What worked? What to try differently? Reflect on the session's activities to identify successes and areas for improvement.
  • Team agreements; follow‑up plan Establish ground rules for the team and set a schedule for future check-ins.

Strategies for Managing Cultural Differences

Clear Norms

Publish response‑time expectations, meeting language, and decision‑making process. Clarify “how we disagree.”

Language Access

Plain English/French; avoid idioms. Share agendas & summaries. Use visuals and written confirmations for decisions.

Bias Checks

Spot biased assumptions. Encourage “call‑in” language: “I noticed… can we revisit phrasing?”

Feedback Frameworks

Use SBI (Situation‑Behavior‑Impact) or NVC. Invite the other person’s perspective; co‑create solutions.

Rotate Facilitation

Share airtime; rotate note‑taker & facilitator roles to reduce hierarchy and increase inclusion.

Repair & Follow‑up

Use “oops/ouch” or quick repair scripts. Document agreements and owners with dates.

Role‑Play

Characters

Jasmine: team leader

A hard-working team leader who motivates other team members. She is good at communicating with the other team members and helps motivate them.

Tyler: team leader 2

A team leader who is disinterested in working. He does not do work and only relies on Jasmine to do the work. He puts strict deadlines, often expecting the developers to finish work within two days or less.

Grace: developer

A developer who is proficient in working alone and with a team. She is able to work on problems easily and helps others if they need help. She has a hard time saying no when it comes to helping others.

Jean: developer

A developer who has a hard time finishing tasks before the deadline. Because of this, she spreads negativity to her team, often lowering team morale. She asks Grace to do the work when she is unable to do it herself. She has no motivation to work, but works to earn money (continuous).

Paul: Boss

A tough boss who is difficult to please. He does not give feedback to employees, nor does he allow them to voice their opinions. He does not ask other employees when it comes to making decisions. Since he has a high position, he feels obligated to continue working (normative) despite not liking his work environment.

Scenarios

Niceties: addressing individual needs

Discuss how Grace may face burnout because she is unable to say no when other people ask her for help

  • Goal:Set work boundaries; make resources availble; team cohesion.
  • Practice:Make boundaries clear in offical communication(?)
Transition: from in-person to online

The pandemic has hit Canadian workspaces hard, but this is an opportunity for growth. Discuss how companies can help support their employees, especially those who are resistant to change work styles, when it comes to transitioning from in person work to online work?

  • Goal: Clarify intent; set language norms; agree on signal if tone feels harsh.
  • Practice: Paraphrasing, I‑statements, ask‑before‑assume.
Miscommunication: language barrier

A new junior programmer has joined the team temporarily from Fiji. English is their second language. Tyler uses informal language like jargon and idioms during team meetings and his slideshows. This makes the junior programmer disconnected with the team. Discuss how this issue can be addressed to help ensure that all members of the team understand instructions clearly.

  • Goal: Clarify intent; set language norms; agree on signal if tone feels harsh.
  • Practice: Paraphrasing, I‑statements, ask‑before‑assume.

Communication Techniques

Nonviolent Communication (NVC)

  1. Observe (no judgments)
  2. Share feeling (“I felt…”)
  3. Name need (e.g., clarity, respect)
  4. Request (concrete, doable)

SBI Framework

  1. Situation: When/where
  2. Behavior: What you did/said
  3. Impact: How it affected me/others

Tip: Use inclusive language; check for understanding; summarize agreements in writing.
